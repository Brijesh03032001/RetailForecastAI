Rossmann Store Sales — Business Context
========================================

COMPANY OVERVIEW
----------------
Rossmann is one of Europe's largest drug store chains, operating over 3,000 stores across
Germany, Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic, Turkey, and Albania. Each store sells a wide
range of personal care, beauty, household, and wellness products. The company competes
closely with dm-drogerie markt and focuses on value pricing with frequent promotions.

STORE TYPES
-----------
Rossmann operates four distinct store formats, each with different characteristics:

Type A — Standard stores: The most common format (~60% of stores). Mid-size urban and
suburban locations with typical weekend and holiday footfall patterns. Average daily
sales range between 4,000 and 8,000 units depending on season.

Type B — Community stores: The smallest format, typically in smaller towns or residential
neighbourhoods. They show weaker promo uplift because the catchment area is smaller. Average
daily sales are 20–35% lower than Type A.

Type C — Larger stores: High-traffic locations, often near transport hubs or shopping
centres. They respond strongly to promotions and have above-average weekend-to-weekday
ratios. Average daily sales are 15–25% above Type A.

Type D — Flagship / destination stores: The largest format, typically in city centres.
Very high baseline sales and the strongest promo response in the chain. Weekend uplift
can be 50–80% above weekday levels.

PROMOTIONAL MECHANICS
---------------------
Rossmann runs two types of promotions that meaningfully lift sales:

Promo (standard): A store-level weekly promotional event with discounts on selected
categories. Standard promos lift unit sales by an average of 20–30% versus non-promo
weeks across the chain. However, the lift is highest for Type C and D stores (30–40%)
and lower for Type B (10–15%). Promo periods also shift purchasing earlier in the week,
causing Monday and Tuesday to outperform Friday–Saturday during active promo weeks.

Promo2 (loyalty programme): A longer-running, subscription-style promotional scheme
that runs for consecutive intervals. Stores enrolled in Promo2 show a sustained 5–10%
baseline lift over non-enrolled stores but less acute weekly spikes. Promo2 enrollment
is voluntary and varies by region.

SEASONALITY AND HOLIDAY EFFECTS
--------------------------------
Rossmann sales follow strong predictable seasonal patterns:

December: The peak trading month. Sales typically run 35–50% above the annual daily
average due to gift products, advent calendars, and seasonal health items. The last
two weeks before Christmas are the highest-volume period. Categories driving this peak
include beauty gifts, candles, skincare sets, and vitamins.

January: The sharpest trough of the year. Post-holiday, sales drop to 15–25% below the
annual average. Consumers are conservative with spending. Store managers should plan
reduced stock orders for the first three weeks of January.

Easter: Germany observes Good Friday and Easter Monday as public holidays. Stores closed
on these days show zero sales. The week before Easter sees a compensatory uplift of
10–15% as consumers stock up.

Summer (July–August): A moderate trough, particularly for indoor-use products. Sun care,
insect repellent, and travel-size products partially offset declines in other categories.
Overall sales 5–10% below spring average.

Public holidays (DE): Stores in German states are subject to state-specific holiday laws.
On public holidays stores must close. The day before a holiday typically shows a 15–20%
uplift (pre-holiday stocking effect).

Day-of-Week Effects
-------------------
Monday: Slightly below weekly average — consumers are back at work after weekend.
Tuesday–Thursday: The core trading days. Stable and predictable.
Friday: The strongest day of the week for most store types. End-of-week top-up shopping
and start-of-weekend stocking drive 15–25% above the weekly average.
Saturday: High for Type C and D stores (near Type A Friday levels). Moderate for Type A
and B as fewer consumers are in shopping destinations.
Sunday: Almost always zero — German law requires stores to be closed on Sundays except
in designated shopping areas and public transport hubs.

COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE
---------------------
dm-drogerie markt is the primary competitor. When dm opens a new store within 500m of
an existing Rossmann store, affected Rossmann stores typically see a 3–8% permanent
baseline sales reduction lasting 6–12 months. Competitive effects are not directly
observable in the sales data but should be considered when forecasts show sustained
unexplained downward drift.

KEY PRODUCT CATEGORIES AND MARGINS
------------------------------------
Personal care (shampoo, shower gel, deodorant): ~28% of revenue. Low margin (~18%),
high volume. Very promo-sensitive.

Beauty and skin care: ~22% of revenue. Higher margin (~35%). Seasonal peak in December
and around Valentine's Day (mid-February). Responds well to Promo2.

Health and pharmacy (OTC vitamins, cold remedies): ~18% of revenue. Margin ~25%.
January through March is the peak cold season with a 20–30% lift in this category.

Household and cleaning: ~15% of revenue. Low margin (~14%). Spring is the peak (March–
May) due to seasonal cleaning behaviour in Germany.

Baby and child care: ~10% of revenue. Stable demand, less cyclical, low promo response.

Other / seasonal: ~7% of revenue. Highly variable depending on season (e.g., sun care in
summer, Christmas decorations in Q4).

FORECASTING NOTES FOR THE AI MODEL
------------------------------------
1. Zero-sales days are almost always caused by store closures (holidays, Sunday) and
   should not be treated as anomalies or imputed — they are structural zeros.

2. Refurbishment / temporary closure: Some stores have extended zero-sales streaks
   (1–4 weeks) due to renovation. These should be excluded from training windows
   or flagged as anomalies.

3. New store ramp-up: Stores in their first 6 months of operation show a ramp-up
   pattern with steadily increasing sales week-over-week. ARIMA models should be
   trained only on post-ramp data (month 7 onward) for such stores.

4. The ARIMA_PLUS model in BigQuery captures trend, seasonality, and holiday effects
   automatically. Supply any additional context (promo flags, store type) as regressors
   in the TRANSFORM clause for best results.

5. Confidence intervals (80% CI) should be communicated as plausible ranges, not hard
   bounds. Actual sales falling outside the CI in consecutive weeks is a signal to
   investigate operational causes (e.g., stock-outs, competitor actions).

6. Executive summaries should avoid alarming language for minor deviations (< 5% off
   forecast). Reserve cautionary language for sustained trends or deviations > 15%.

DATA DICTIONARY (rossmann_cleaned.csv)
----------------------------------------
date         : ISO 8601 date (YYYY-MM-DD)
product_id   : Store identifier in the format STORE_XXXX (e.g., STORE_0001)
store_id     : Store type code — a, b, c, or d (mapped from StoreType in raw data)
units_sold   : Integer count of items sold that day (0 on closed days)
revenue      : Total revenue in EUR for that day (0 on closed days)
